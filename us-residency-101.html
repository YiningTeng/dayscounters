US Residency 101

Overview

Navigating the US residency system is crucial for understanding your immigration status, tax obligations, and eligibility for benefits. This guide will walk you through the key types of US residency, tax implications, and official resources.

Types of Residency

  • Nonresident Alien: A non-US citizen who does not meet the substantial presence test or hold a green card.
    • Tax: Taxed only on US-sourced income.
    • Examples: Visitors or students on short-term visas.
  • Resident Alien: A non-US citizen who meets the substantial presence test or holds a green card.
    • Tax: Taxed on worldwide income, similar to citizens.
    • Examples: Long-term visa holders.
  • US Citizen: Born in the US, or naturalized, with all associated legal rights.
    • Tax: Must report income worldwide, even while living abroad.
    • Examples: Dual citizens.

Substantial Presence Test

To qualify as a resident alien under the substantial presence test, you must:

  • Be in the US for at least 31 days in the current year.
  • Be in the US for 183 days across the past three years (using weighted calculations).

Detailed information can be found on the IRS website.

Permanent Residency (Green Card)

  • Lawful permanent resident status, allowing indefinite living and working in the US.
  • PR holders must file taxes on worldwide income.
  • Details available on USCIS.

Tax Implications

  • Worldwide Taxation: US residents and citizens report income globally.
  • Forms: Use Form 1040 (resident) or 1040-NR (nonresident).
  • Learn more on the IRS website.
